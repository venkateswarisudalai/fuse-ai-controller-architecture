Failure walk-throughs (interactive on the web version)

F1 — Cloud dies / plan goes stale

  1. The plan watchdog at the site edge expires — no fresh schedule from the cloud.
  2. Chargers revert to the pre-staged default: charge toward departure, fair share within the cap, never export. Positioned before the outage, not computed during it.
  3. The site cap is enforced locally — it never depended on the cloud. Certified hardware sits below even that.
  4. Outcome: no vehicle stranded because the brain died. Grid services pause; fleet operations don't.

F2 — Utility demands response in 60 seconds

  1. A DERMS dispatch lands mid-interval: shed 400 kW, starting in 60 seconds.
  2. The edge responds immediately from pre-planned headroom — headroom exists because the day-ahead bid was conservative (~P10).
  3. In parallel, an out-of-cycle MPC re-solve re-plans the rest of the day. The solver is never on the 60-second path.
  4. New commands re-enter through the rules gate — revalidated against live state, epoch-fenced so stale commands are dropped.
  5. Outcome: dispatch delivered and measured — delivered ÷ committed kWh per event. That's the ≥90% metric, with evidence.

F3 — Impossible night: no valid plan exists

  1. The fleet arrives two hours late with low batteries. Departure charge + site cap + tonight's commitment cannot all hold.
  2. The solver returns an infeasible subset (IIS): the 400 kW commitment, 12 departures, and the 600 kW cap cannot all hold. An AI loop would have iterated and timed out.
  3. Relaxation runs bottom-up: the utility commitment is partially curtailed at its real tariff-penalty price. Departures win.
  4. Outcome: the operator gets a named conflict, not a mystery — and every relaxation is logged.

Optimizer — math decides

Every hard limit is a constraint the solver must satisfy simultaneously — or it reports exactly which ones conflict.

Why a solver, not the AI: every hard constraint here is linear or piecewise-linear, so a MILP satisfies them provably — and when no valid plan exists, it returns an irreducible infeasible subset (IIS) — a minimal set of constraints that cannot all hold. An AI loop can only time out.

Rules gate — the deterministic boundary

Nothing reaches a charger without crossing this line.

Why two checks: a plan validated at 4:00 can be unsafe at 4:07 — a van unplugged, building load spiked. So the gate checks the plan and re-checks every command at the moment it's issued.

Site edge

A small computer at each depot. No AI, no optimizer, bounded latency.

The fallback insight: a fallback isn't computed during an outage — it's pre-positioned before one. Even a fully compromised cloud cannot overload a site.

Why not AI-in-a-loop?

My first sketch: AI proposes a schedule → rules engine flags violations → AI re-proposes → repeat until compliant.

  • No infeasibility certificate. The loop can't tell “not found yet” from “doesn't exist” — it burns iterations and gives up. A solver returns the conflicting constraint set (IIS).
  • Oscillation where the money is. With tight coupled constraints (site cap + commitment + departures — the revenue regime), fixing one violation creates another.
  • The fallback paradox. A fallback good enough to hold ≥90% dispatch success is a deterministic scheduler. You'd maintain two planners and trust one.
Verdict: rejected as the primary mechanism — the problem is a textbook constrained optimization.
What survived: a bounded ≤2-pass repair step at the rules gate, as an escape hatch for formulation gaps. For v1 I'd consider cutting even that.

Assumptions & positions taken

These are business-shaped calls. I took a position on each rather than leave the design underspecified; each is validated with real data, not opinion.

1 · Constraint shedding order
Position: departure SoC outranks utility commitments — stranding a van costs more than a dispatch penalty. Validated against actual tariff penalty structures and fleet SLAs.
2 · Warranty enforcement acceptance
Assumption: “software-enforced warranty compliance” eventually needs written OEM acceptance of the enforcement logic. Until then, the per-VIN ledger is the evidence base.
3 · Human override semantics
Position: overrides go through the gate, never around it. Which role unlocks which tier (site manager vs fleet HQ) is deployment configuration, not architecture.
4 · Commitment aggressiveness
Assumption: the pilot bids conservatively (~P10 flexibility) and moves up the revenue/reliability curve as M&V data accumulates.
